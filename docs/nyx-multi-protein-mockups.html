Nyx · design exploration · B-351

Every protein in the bowl, not just the one on the front of the bag

A food carries more than one protein. A “duck” formula that also lists chicken by-product meal is the textbook reason a home elimination trial silently fails — and today we only store the primary, so that chicken is invisible to every clinical surface.

These mocks show how capturing the full protein set reads for an owner, and how attribution stays honest when proteins can’t yet be separated.

4 surfaces · picker · trial catch · Signal · vet report Spec: nyx-multi-protein-requirements.md Status: provisional — pending D1 ratification

Dr. Chen — capture all

Sensitivity

Hidden secondary proteins are the #1 reason a home trial silently fails. You can’t detect a contaminant you never recorded.

Data Scientist — counterweight

Attribution

Co-occurring proteins are collinear — capturing more can muddy which protein is the culprit and bloat the exposure set.

The resolution these mocks embody: capture everything (wins exposure outright) — and when two proteins always travel together, name them jointly rather than crediting one falsely.

  • 1

    Main is its own line — editing it is one tap

    The single-select Main protein reuses the shipped B-332 picker unchanged (= proteins[0]); Also contains holds the secondaries. Change the main by tapping a new chip; the previous main auto-slides into “Also contains,” so the set is never lost and you never “remove all and re-tap in order.”

  • 2

    The split matches the clinical model

    The main is what the food is sold as (and the trial protein); the secondaries are the hidden ones that break trials. The two lines literally are the two jobs — exposure vs. what-it’s-built-around.

  • 3

    Never null-clobber an AI value

    Both lines reseed from the stored array and only write on a tap — an AI-hydrated set is never silently overwritten. A wrong extraction is corrected, not fought.

  • 4

    Owner-picked and AI-extracted key identically

    Every chip flows through the same canonicalizeProtein — an owner tap and a model label can never fragment (B-332 parity, extended to the set). Factual, never preference (B-112). nyx-voice pass.

2The contaminant catchPhase A · PR 4 · the wedge win

The highest-value slice is deterministic — no correlation to accumulate, no attribution ambiguity. When a pet is on a declared elimination trial and a food’s protein set contains a protein the trial should exclude, flag it the moment the data exists: at the confirm screen, and as a standing note on the diet-trial card.

3Honest attribution on the SignalPhase B · PR 5 · adversarial-mandatory

This is the Data Scientist’s guardrail made visible. When two proteins always appear together in what the pet eats, the engine can’t separate them — so it names them jointly and says so, rather than crediting one falsely. The moment the diet varies (one fed without the other), attribution sharpens on its own.

  • G

    Collinear proteins → one joint candidate

    Proteins that always co-occur are counted as one cluster — which also keeps the Bonferroni family sized by discriminating clusters, not raw protein count. Kills the “4–5 proteins bloat the family” objection.

  • G

    Never credit one over its twin

    The copy names both and says why they can’t be split. Associational only — “track with,” never “cause.” The existing validatePhrasing guardrails hold.

  • 1

    Set-membership, not primary-only

    The hidden secondary now enters the exposure set — the sensitivity win. Omnipresent staples still wash out (B-070); a protein that discriminates earns clean attribution.

  • G

    The diet varying is the unlock

    The card points at the one action that separates them. This is honest uncertainty guiding a next step — never reassurance about the other protein.
