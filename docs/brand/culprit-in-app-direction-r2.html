§3 · your reaction #5 You said:

The night moment, in one sentence

When a wait is full-screen — the app has nothing else to show you yet — the wait itself becomes a branded screen: the night, the Whorl, one warm line. That's the whole idea. The WhorlSpinner (§2) is a small element inside a screen that already has content; the night moment is what fills the screen when there is no content to stand behind — first hydration on a new device, building the vet report, reading a photo. What qualifies — the hard rule

All three must be true: the wait blocks the whole screen (nothing useful to show behind it), it's expected to exceed ~2 seconds, and the app is doing real work on the pet's behalf.

The three, honestly compared

  • A · Dawn horizon. The page's top region — status bar, header, Signal — is the sky, fading to daylight before the Today card. There is no edge to feel because the transition is a gradient, not a border. Costs: the header goes moonlight (bigger change), and the pinned-header scroll behavior needs a design decision (the sky can scroll away or stay). The most branded outcome, and the aurora finally lives where you loved it — at the top.
  • B · Dusk card. Round 1's card, lifted one step lighter (#2B2660 ground) with the aurora at half strength. Same layout as today, smallest diff, ships fastest. The cliff becomes a step — present, but polite.
  • C · Tinted canvas. The page ground shifts #FAFAFA → #F6F5FB (an indigo-leaning off-white) so the room itself leans night. Alone it's too subtle to fix the cliff; as a base coat under A or B it makes either sit better. Cheap, reversible.

Contrast receipts on the dusk ground #2B2660: moonlight text 11.7:1, muted 5.6:1, teal 6.0:1, lifted rose 5.0:1 — every pair holds WCAG AA (computed, not eyeballed; the deeper gradient end scores higher still). In RN, A's card translucency is a solid overlay tint (no backdrop blur — that's web garnish here, not load-bearing). Recommendation: A + C together, B held as the fallback if the horizon reads heavy on-device.

The briefing, across three kinds of day

The idea only proves itself in motion: same pet, same rules, three different mornings. Switch the scenario and watch which cards earn their place — and which stay home. The gate is always "does this card carry information today," never a layout count.

What the scenarios demonstrate

  • A rough morning: the chronicity safety finding leads on its rail, Today shows the 6am episodes, the evening dose is due — five cards, all load-bearing. Nothing was "added"; the day earned them.
  • An ordinary day: the trial tick and a calm pattern — four cards, quieter.
  • A healthy pet (no meds, no trial): exactly today's three zones — Signal, Today, Trend. The briefing model contains the current design as its simplest case. Nobody's home gets busier than their pet's actual day.
  • Safety cards are never gated, never reordered below the fold — the revised Principle 3 rule carries over verbatim.

Still not ratified — D9 asks whether to adopt the model, and the Tier-2 §3 principles edit ships with that decision. The Care card's copy stays factual ("evening dose · usually given with food"), one per day max, never a push.

The product team, consulted

Data Scientist"The six-month strip shows exact day-counts per month — numbers, not shades, so there's no quiz and no false trend line. And the coverage tick matters clinically: an empty June with no ticks is unlogged, not well. The two must never read the same."
Dr. Chen — veterinarian"'Mar 1 · Apr 2 · May 8 · Jun 5' is the chronicity picture I ask owners to reconstruct from memory in the exam room. If the owner can show me that strip, that's a better history than most referrals. Keep the numbers exact; never summarize months into a vibe."
Sam — cat owner"On the worst weeks I don't want the calendar to shout. Dots stay small, empty days stay plain, and the summary line tells me the truth in one sentence I could read at 6am."
QA"Cells hold the 44pt floor at device scale; the layer toggle is one switch, off by default; the strip is six buttons, each labeled for VoiceOver ('May, 8 days with vomiting')."

Also explored and parked: symptom-type color coding per pip (multiple hues = a new quiz — the drill-in already answers "what kind"); medication-day markers on the grid (real confounder value, but it belongs to the correlation engine's story — routed to the Signal/report, not the calendar's v2); a full-screen calendar surface (the card covers the month; a dedicated screen only earns itself once range controls exist — noted for the detail-screen follow-up, not v2).

Three beats, all quiet

  • The landing arc (shown): check → ping → the dot arcs to the Home tab and settles with one glint. It literally deposits the log where the intelligence lives — and primes the header mark's pulse if the log completes a finding.
  • The trial tick: during an active diet trial the completion line adds one factual clause — "Day 12 of 28 — logged." Progress stated, never gamified: no streaks, no flames, no guilt mechanics (Principle 4 — a missed day costs nothing and is never mentioned).
  • Tone discipline unchanged: symptom logs keep the calm beat — check, ping, arc, no gold. The celebrate glow still belongs to genuinely earned moments only.

~1.4s total including the arc; the arc respects reduced-motion (dot simply appears on the tab). RN: one Animated value driving translate along a quadratic — native driver, no layout thrash.
