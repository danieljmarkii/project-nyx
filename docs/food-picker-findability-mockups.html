Finding a food in the picker

Household feedback (2026-07-15): logging from FAB → Recent is quick, but when the food isn't in Recent, picking it out of the long library list is hard. Photos made the Foods tab easier to scan — what would make the picker's library findable?

Three independent levers below, each mocked against today's picker, plus the composite we recommend. React to any of them — they're separable PRs.

Surface FoodPicker library zone (app/log.tsx → components/log/FoodPicker.tsx) Explored by Sr. Product Designer, synced with the team Date 2026-07-15

Where the friction actually is

Before proposing anything, the team walked the failure case: the food you want isn't in Recent, so you scroll. Four separate things make that scroll slow — and only one of them is "no photos."

  • Recent is capped at 5 foods from the last 14 days, in a horizontal scroll. A rotation of 8–12 foods means the one you want just missed the cut — constantly. This is likely the single biggest contributor to falling into the long list at all.
  • Search sits mid-screen, below Add-new + Recent + Always available. Already filed as B-020. The fastest findability tool in the picker is the one you have to scroll to reach.
  • Every library tile is the same white rectangle. Text-only tiles mean scanning = reading every label. The redesign doc's own premise (§1): image recognition ~13 ms vs ~250 ms to parse a text row. Photos were deliberately retired from the picker in May 2026 — more on that under Option A, because the reason they were retired has since been solved.
  • The list itself is longer than it should be. Duplicate foods (B-009, dedup spec drafted and awaiting ratification) pad the list. Not a picker-UI fix, but every duplicate row is findability tax.

Option CThe shelf that holds the whole rotation

Cheapest · likely fixes the actual reported case Small build

The quiet insight from the team sync: your wife's friction starts before the long list — it starts when the food she wants isn't in Recent. Recent holds 5 foods from 14 days; a real household rotation is 8–12. Widen the shelf to hold the rotation, and the long list becomes a rare destination instead of a daily one.

RecommendedThe composite — C, then B, then A

Team recommendation Three small PRs, in that order

The three options attack different failure points and compose without fighting: the rotation shelf absorbs the everyday case, pinned search catches the known-name case, photos make the true browse case scannable. Sequenced cheapest-first, so value lands even if we stop early.

Suggested sequence — three independent PRs

  • PR 1 — Rotation shelf (Option C). Smallest diff, highest odds of dissolving the reported friction. Ships alone; measurable immediately in the household.
  • PR 2 — Pinned search + scope chips (Option B). Closes B-020. Independent files from PR 1's shelf work aside from FoodPicker itself — sequence, don't parallelize, to avoid rebasing the same component.
  • PR 3 — Tile thumbnails (Option A). The biggest visual change and the one carrying a decision reversal — gated on your ratification below. Extracting the shared thumbnail component also positions B-114's monogram for the Foods tab.
Product OwnerBacklog reconciliation if ratified: B-020 closes with PR 2; B-114 folds into PR 3; B-007 (FAB revamp) and B-019 stay open — this exploration doesn't touch the FAB itself. B-009 dedup remains the orthogonal "shorter list" half, still awaiting its own ratification.
QAEach PR re-runs the §10 acceptance bar: 10-second test on an already-logged food, one-handed, unchanged or better.

What we deliberately didn't propose

  • Full-width photo rows in the picker (the Foods-tab layout). It's the layout your wife finds pleasant — but it halves candidate density on the hot path and reopens B-113, which you ruled as Option C (keep the tile/row split, harmonize structure). Small thumbs on tiles capture most of the recognition benefit without the density cost.
  • Favorites / "loved foods" shelf in the picker. Intake is not preference — a finished bowl next to a one-tap-log control must never read as "she loves this" (the B-112 line). The rotation shelf is recency-factual instead.
  • Alphabet index rail. Right tool for a 500-item contact list, overkill for a personal library of 20–60 foods with brand grouping already in place.
  • Reordering the library by frequency. Frecency sorting churns spatial memory — the food that was "bottom left under Tiki Cat" yesterday shouldn't teleport overnight. The rotation shelf gives recency a home without destabilizing the browse list.
